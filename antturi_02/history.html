History of Blue

Blue, a primary color, seen above in our sky, and below in our seas, holds a wide range of applications and representations both practically and thematically. Historically, blue had been desirable and symbolic amongst royalty, stemming from rare blue minerals and dyes. Following World War II, the color has been gendered to be associated most closely with males. This can be seen commonly today in childrens clothing, toys, and signage.

Blue as a feeling

In art blue has long been used as a theme to represent a number of tones and emotions. Emotionally, the color is most closely associated with feelings of sadness and depression. The expression, “Feeling Blue”, believed to have emerged in the 17th century, is a popular idiom in the English language today. Artists and musicians alike have often used this blue association to viscerally communicate a deep and intense sadness, from Vincent Van Gogh to Miles Davis and Joni Mitchell.

Blue in practical use

In our everyday lives, blue plays many roles through symbols and associations. In the United States, blue has become the color representing of law enforcement and medical uniforms alike. As a color, it presents trustworthiness and stability. For jobs of service the blue is meant to evoke a sense of credability and assurance.

The color blue is typically associated with credibility, trust, knowledge, power, professionalism, cleanliness, calm and focus. Because all of these qualities are valued in the medical community, blue is the ideal choice for medical logos. - Doctor Multimedia

Blue in sports

Blue of all shades can be found as the main color of teams across all sports. In New York specifically, the color is prominent across several sports. The Yankees, Giants, Knicks, Rangers, and Mets all share heavy use of blue. For fans across rivalry lines, differences in shades of blue make all the difference. The dark navy blue of the Yankees vs the lighter Mets blue represents a contrast in loyalties, association, and identity.
